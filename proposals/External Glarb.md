## Why Glarb?

Why Glarb?

![My Favorite GIF](https://media1.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExcmplcGJqMXVzZHJhMWMweDh2aHQyazA2MzVwZHhsZ2k3dHh2empjciZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/ADZspwe7WzwlXMXFIU/giphy.gif)

Aside from being a cutie-patootie, [Glarb, Calamity's Augur](about:blank) acts as a fantastic engine piece that:

1. Fixes your draws
2. Fills your graveyard
3. Gives you access to more cards from the top of your library (a pseudo-draw if you will)
4. Incidentally acts as a fantastic blocker (deathtouch + 4 toughness puts in a LOT of work)

What I really enjoy in commander is a feeling of consistency but not repetitiveness, having a high floor but also a high ceiling. I wanted this deck to always be able to do something and have a variety of ways to close out games. To do so, I felt that I needed to have a low-cost commander with built in draw that was some variation of Green and Blue (because we all know Simic is the best color combination in commander). Green for all your land ramp/dork shenanigans and Blue for draw and interaction. Also Green for artifact and enchantment removal, and also for some recursion (through [regrowth](about:blank) effects). Also Green for creature tutors. Yeah turns out Green in casual is pretty good. I also really liked big mana Black/Dimir spells, which ultimately led me to Glarb.

Glarb is a very passive card advantage engine and will give you a LOT of value over the course of the game without drawing a lot of attention. While Glarb doesn't explicitly have an ability that lets you draw, the ability to fix the top card of your library and [future sight](about:blank) (play off the top) is arguably better, as I would rather draw into something I want and can use than something I don't. We all know how much it sucks to top-deck into something useless late game, so being able to avoid this (or prepare your next turn in advance) is really strong. This ability really shines when you couple it with top-deck tutors like [Worldly Tutor](about:blank), [Vampiric Tutor](about:blank), and [Sylvan Tutor](about:blank), allowing you to essentially bypass the restriction of these tutor effect (note that all of these are missing from this deck but are very easy inclusions).

In the same breadth, being able to surveil 2 each turn lets you sculpt your perfect graveyard as a "secondary hand" (albeit not to the extent of a repeated [entomb](about:blank) or [buried alive](about:blank)). Nothing sucks more than drawing something like an [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank) on turn 4 without any way to cast it, so being able to pitch it away for later access via a [Reanimate](about:blank) or [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) is always helpful. It may feel awkward pitching a [counterspell](about:blank) from the top of your library to find something else, but you'll have access to plenty of card recursion to get back key pieces when needed. This ability also synergizes very well with other "self-mill matters" pieces, like [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank) and [Polluted Cistern](about:blank), giving you an alternative burn based win condition in addition to your other "graveyard slop".

However, all of this isn't even the most most broken part of Glarb. In my opinion, what's busted is being able to play lands off the top of your library. Being able to consistently hit your land drops each turn is invaluable and will also help "thin" your deck, giving you more gas each turn. When you combine this with abilities that let you play extra lands each turn (like [Exploration](about:blank), [Icetill Explorer](about:blank), and [Prismatic Undercurrents](about:blank)), you'll be able to churn through lands with ease and always find something to do.

And last but not least, Glarb works as a great rattlesnake (a deterrent that stops others from attacking into you). It's surprising how much damage you'll be able to avoid from 1/X, 2/X, and 3/X creatures since opponents do not want to trade them away. Conversely, you'll find some smart people who recognize the importance of Glarb and call your bluff, swinging 4+ power creatures at you since they know you won't block. But the option is always there and will save you a lot of life in the long run.

## What does this Deck do? What's the appeal?

What does this Deck do? What's the appeal?

![My Favorite GIF](https://media0.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExM3Rjd3Rjemk3MHJ2enRsZ2xiN2g1ZWx0MW9uaGl1dzN1Zzd1dGVnNyZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/8OgZyXwljMPo1Cl4dg/giphy.gif)

This deck looks to setup, stabilize, and win by either:

1. Combo. There's line with [Polluted Cistern](about:blank), [Doom Whisperer](about:blank), and [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank) that's specifically included to help close out games and give you the win. Can explicitly look for this during the mid game if you have the pieces for it, will get into more detail below. Note that this is pseudo-deterministic depending on how many cards you have left in your library relative to each opponent’s life total (gets easier when you have something like [Endurance](about:blank) is hand as well).
2. Drains via [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank). As you'll see in the deck list, you have a lot of big, scary, and Black-pip heavy 5+ MV bombs that will shift the game in your favor, and you'll find a typically Gary drop to swing the life total by 7 each in your favor. Plus, this deck uses your life a lot as a resource, so having some more incidental lifegain is always great. Also combines really well with clones like [Phyrexian Metamorph](about:blank) and [Clever Impersonator](about:blank).
3. Burn via [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank) and [Polluted Cistern](about:blank). There are quite a few pieces in your deck that help you just dump your library into your graveyard, and you'll find it easy to burn people out in games. Couple this with bombs like [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) to deal big swings of damage. You can also fill up your graveyard and nuke people out by exiling it with [Boggart Trawler](about:blank).
4. Attrition. At worst, this deck becomes your stock-standard Sultai slop pile, able to grind out, out value, and out-resource your opponents. You will find that this deck is incredibly resilient, able to beat down opponents with stuff like [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank) and find silver bullets like [Massacre Wurm](about:blank). Plus, dropping down spells like [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) and [Jetsam](about:blank) is usually enough to win you the game in the following turns depending on what is in people's graveyards. You will always have something to do and enough lifegain to turn games back around.

My goal for this list was to have something that I felt was fun and "fair" to play as and against (personal note: apparently people do NOT feel the same when playing against this but whatever, at least I'M having fun).

As for deck restrictions and considerations, I wanted to build this WITHOUT any gamechangers and to keep the price around $750-800 (using cheapest printings) if possible (but hey what's another $10 amirite). I don't have any beef with gamechangers, and I promise there are a few that this deck would go crazy for (looking at you [Seedborn Muse](about:blank) and [The One Ring](about:blank)). If it feels like there's an obvious include that would work great, but it costs like $50 then there you have it. Feel free to upgrade/make changes as you see fit.

## Card-by-Card Breakdown (by category)

Ramp

![My Favorite GIF](https://media1.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExdXI5c2tnYnU4azZvdnd6dmZ2b2EzZmRtMHB3dnNsdXJibWowMHVxbiZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/QTc4NvhYNlyT7Q32uj/giphy.gif)

While one of the biggest appeals of Green is that it has 2 MV ramp like [Nature's Lore](about:blank) and [Three Visits](about:blank) and 3 MV ramp like [Cultivate](about:blank) and [Kodama's reach](about:blank), I don't think that most 2 or 3 MV ramp is a good fit for this deck.

Any deck that wants to get their commander out a turn early needs to have ramp that makes sense. A 5 MV commander needs 3 mana ramp to be dropped on turn 4, a 4 MV commander needs 2 mana ramp to be dropped on turn 3, and a 3 MV commander needs 1 mana ramp to be dropped on turn 2. Plus, you also have to think about WHY you want to ramp. Having more mana is always great, but it helps to think about what you can do with all of that mana. Since Glarb lets you fill your graveyard and fix your draws each turn, getting him out a turn early only serves to improve the quality of your graveyard and future turns. And since we have a significant amount of 4+ MV spells we want to cast, we want to hit our 8 mana threshold so we can start double-spell'ing each turn or cast a spell and hold up mana for interaction. I've found from testing that you can and want to do a LOT at flash speed.

Since Glarb specifically costs , we're looking for 1 mana ramp that fixes colors to get Glarb out a turn early, and then subsequent 4+ MV ramp afterwards to be able to speed up our gameplan. Don't forget that Glarb lets us play lands off the top, so having a critical amount of extra-land options lets us accelerate each turn more than a simple [Farseek](about:blank). This compounds each turn as well, so you'll often find yourself with like 10 lands by turn 6, allowing you to double spell with mid-range bombs and close out games. Nothing is scarier than dropping [Sheoldred](about:blank) into [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank) to drain each person for 4 and gain 12 (at minimum). Note that this is NOT a landfall or lands matters deck, but it can easily be pivoted into such.

1 MV Ramp:

- [Birds of Paradise](about:blank): not much to say here, one of the best dorks in the format. One option you can slot into this deck later on is [Valley Floodcaller](about:blank), which will untap this creature upon casting a non-creature spell.
- [Deathrite Shaman](about:blank): one of the best 1-drops in the entire game. 2 toughness to protect against pings, premier graveyard hate, burn, and lifegain. Works better as a dork if you have more fetch lands, but that's an easy upgrade for down the line. Also scales in power with the strength of your pod since higher power decks tend to run more fetch lands.
- [Delighted Halfling](about:blank): another great 1-drop, again 2 toughness to help against pings and gives you a [Cavern of Souls](about:blank) effect for your legendaries.
- [Elves of Deep Shadow](about:blank): nothing crazy here, the advantage of this over something like [Llanowar Elves](about:blank) is that it essentially converts your green mana to black mana to avoid issues with color pips.
- [Carpet of Flowers](about:blank): while the floor of this is a 1-mana do nothing, the ceiling is absolutely insane. Scales very well with pod power as higher power decks typically run some variation of blue in them, and it can also dissuade opponents from playing islands to stop you from ramping (I promise this comes up more than you think). You can very easily find yourself making excess of 4 mana from this during the mid game depending on who you play against, and the ability to have it activate on your second main phase also gives you flexibility as you aren't locked to dropping everything pre-combat.
- [Exploration](about:blank): MVP of this deck, works fantastic with Glarb and lets you just churn out a stupid amount of lands. Playing this on T1 lets you get up to 6 lands on T3, and being able to double-land off the top of your library will let you always hit gas each turn.
- [Sol Ring](about:blank): Best piece of mana in the whole game. This doesn't let you get Glarb out a turn early, but it does help you accelerate out the rest of your deck. Having access to 4 mana on Turn 2 is crazy, and any sort of early value creature like [Talion, the Kindly Lord](about:blank) or [Uro, Titan of Nature's Wrath](about:blank) just sets you so far ahead in tempo.

3 MV Ramp:

- [Enduring Vitality](about:blank): Okay after everything I said about 1 MV and 4 MV ramp, this card still feels worth the slot. Yes it does compete with casting Glarb, but the acceleration in mana you get in unreal with this out on the field. Not only is it a decent blocker/attacker at 3/3 with vigilance, but the ability to have your other creatures tap for mana just lets you snowball into so much on your turn. You can convert any creatures that tend to just sit back and block (stuff like [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank), [High Fae Trickster](about:blank), etc.) into extra mana on your turn or on other player's turns, and coupled with your flash enablers gives you a lot more windows to interact and develop. And this is all before the interaction with [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank). With [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank) and [Enduring Vitality](about:blank) in play, each turn cycle you get 3 2/2 mana dorks, which you can then tap to manifest dread themselves. And best of all, if your board gets wipes or if Bambi dies, then it just comes back as an enchantment. This card genuinely sets you ahead of tempo in a way that is unmatched, and in my games so far it has deserved the slot.
- [Dryad of the Ilysian Grove](about:blank): While not castable off the top with Glarb, this creature still does put in a lot of work in giving you extra land drops and fixing your mana. Decent early blocker at 2/4, but the main appeal is that it lets you ramp out with extra lands off the top/from your hand earlier than normal.

4+ MV Ramp:

- [Icetill Explorer](about:blank): another banger, gives you basically everything you need for Glarb. Extra land each turn is great, playing fetches from graveyards is great, and incidental milling to remove something stuck on top of your library is also great. Plus, 4 toughness is surprisingly hard to chew through.
- [Prismatic Undercurrents](about:blank): This has quickly become a personal favorite of mine. Dropping this on Turn 4 after a Turn 3 Glarb lets you fetch out 3 basics from your library (which also thins your library for more gas), shuffles your library to clear your top deck, and affords you an extra land that turn (and for subsequent turns). Absolute fantastic stabilizing piece and guarantees your 2 land drops for the next turn as well. This isn't the most flashy or explosive piece, but it has put in a lot of work.
- [Hedge Shredder](about:blank): a very fun and unorthodox option for Glarb, turns your surveils and mills into ramp. Pair this with [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) and you'll hear a LOT of groans from across the table.

Card Advantage

Card draw, what's not to love? I know there's a sentiment that if you have card draw in your command zone (and if your commander is not like 6 MV) you can get away with lower amounts of draw, but I still think you need a healthy amount of ways to put cards in your hand.

With [Glarb, Calamity's Augur](about:blank) out in the field you can _kind of_ get away with having only a few cards in hand since you can basically just access whatever is on top of your library. However, it is still important to have a significant amount of draw since you need to be able to cast interaction and hold things up for later turns. Plus Glarb is just a creature and is always susceptible to removal/wipes, and you don't want to be in a spot where you're hellbent searching for answers.

As for what _flavor_ of card draw, you have some options depending on what you consider card draw, or _"card advantage"_. Since you're able to put things in your graveyard via Glarb's surveil ability, you also have recursion to think about as means of drawing cards (can check this out in depth in the "Recursion" section). Otherwise, I've added some generic options for card draw and some other options that synergize with Glarb/the graveyard.

Normal Draw (kind of good-stuff heavy but whatever):

- [Sylvan Library](about:blank): Fantastic option for this deck, lets you check out your next 2 cards in advance, re-order how you want, and also pay 4 life for each card you want to draw. Note that you CAN look at the top card of your library after drawing the additional 2, which will help you decide if you want to pay the extra 4 life to draw.
- [Wan Shi Tong, Librarian](about:blank): Ahh [Shit Lib](about:blank), what's not to love? Generically good draw piece that can be casted off the top for X=2. Also turns into a great beater when moderately invested into, the combination of Flying and Vigilance (and dropping it in on the end step before yours) is a great way to start chunking out opponents.
- [Uro, Titan of Nature's Wrath](about:blank): slow but consistent draw engine, lets you also ramp and stabilize on ETB/attack. Since you get to see what you draw ahead of time, you can set it up to ensure you draw what you need rather than something useless (i.e. surveiling 2 in response to the ETB/attack trigger, and then re-organizing the top of your library for what you need). This also helps you convert anything middling in your graveyard to overall advantage, as the escape cost is really easy to pay for in the mid game. And if it gets removed, you just escape it again!
- [Talion, the Kindly Lord](about:blank): can cast off the top with Glarb, but otherwise just a solid card advantage engine. If it ain't broke don't fix it.
- [The Unagi of Kyoshi Island](about:blank): If [Faerie Mastermind](about:blank) and [Consecrated Sphinx](about:blank) had a baby. I know people have mixed opinions about this, but I think this is an absolute bomb (in a good way). It costs , so it can be cast off the top with Glarb (AND at instant speed since it has flash). The fact that it also has Waterbend 4 means that it's hard to remove and will stick around for a while, and the 5/5 stat line means it can dish out damage as well. Plus, it can be fetched with [Waterlogged Teachings](about:blank), letting you turn a middling MDFC into a great draw engine. You'll also run into some people who will intentionally restrict _when_ they draw to stop you from drawing (i.e. hold up their draw spell for a less opportune time to deny you your draw 2 trigger), which puts them behind in tempo. All around fantastic draw option, highly recommend everyone gives this a try.

Graveyard/Niche Draw:

- [Ripples of Undeath](about:blank): note that this triggers on your precombat main phase, NOT your draw/upkeep, so you will still draw the card that's on top of your library before this triggers. Fantastic way to fill up your graveyard and improve the quality of your draws at the expense of life/mana/giving information. But at 2 mana (and an enchantment, means that it's red/black proof xd) it's hard to find something better than this.
- [Palantir of Orthanc](about:blank): Straight pondering it. From my experience playing with this and against this, the typical sentiment is that for the first 3 or so triggers opponents will make you mill cards and will take damage, and then after that they will see the opportunity cost is too big and will let you draw in fear of taking like 15 damage. You can absolutely work this to your benefit and stack the top of your library with big MV spells to chunk people out early game, and then pivot to stuff you want to draw in the mid game once you get to like 3 influence counters. This piece will occasionally attract removal, but it's better to think of this as a way to dig through your library and damage opponents for the first 3 turns of this out, and then as a draw piece afterwards.
- [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank): Army in a can. While not being "direct" card draw, you can churn through so much of your deck and put key creatures onto the battlefield that it functionally acts as draw. Plus, you can easily cheat this out with [Chord of Calling](about:blank) or [Nature's Rhythm](about:blank) to avoid the additional exile 6 cost. And don't even get me started on special actions, this can really surprise someone when you turn your innocent 2/2 into [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](about:blank). And all of this stapled on a 5/5 with flying, what's not to love? The ONLY consideration is that this will involuntarily get rid of your top deck, so specifically fixing it isn't really an option. Having said that, the pros still significantly outweigh the cons and this has been an absolute MVP is stabilization and developing my boardstate. This also doubles as a "budget" [Doom Whipserer](about:blank), letting you dig through cards at the expense of speed rather than life (only 6 cards a turn cycle). Very useful when you're getting beat down and need to stabilize. Don't forget that you can also turn the 2/2's into extra juice for your [Chord of Calling](about:blank) or harmonized [Nature's Rhythm](about:blank) (although you'd usually use those to grab this in the first place xd).

Recursion

![My Favorite GIF](https://media4.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExaDRyNG1oM2h3d2VjaWhvNmtmOWx3dzV1azhqYjQ0aXVqazd0a2ZtZyZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/UMViizH92h6U4EEhZF/giphy.gif)

The biggest thing I wanted to highlight here was that in earlier iterations of this deck, I had quite a bit more reanimation-style effects (specifically including options like [Animate Dead](about:blank), [Necromancy](about:blank), and [Muldrotha, the Gravetide](about:blank)). What I found was that this deck didn't run enough top-end bombs to justify the reanimation spells, and from gameplay wanted more [regrowth](about:blank) effects like [Eternal Witness](about:blank) and [Timeless Witness](about:blank) to get key instants and sorceries back to hand as well as important creatures (but not so much as to run something like [Archaeomancer](about:blank)).

I've specifically chosen and refined my options for recursion to be as lean as possible and give as much flexibility as needed. For this deck, the graveyard is more of an incidental pile that you can take advantage of rather than a large focal point, that way when you get [Bojuka Bog](about:blank)'d it doesn't feel like you've been blown out of the water. As mentioned above, you more want to pick-and-choose what you send to the graveyard rather than just dump everything in.

Generic Recursion:

- [Noxious Revival](about:blank): great piece for this deck, can pay a measly 2 life to hate out someone's key piece or to bring back something to the top of your library, which with Glarb out means you can recast. For example, someone tries to [Counterspell](about:blank) you, so you [Noxious Revival](about:blank) the [Mindbreak Trap](about:blank) in your graveyard to counter their counter. Possibilities are endless, has been a great include.
- [Colossal Skyturtle](about:blank): Another solid option, the regrowth effect is a little more mana expensive than we'd like but it more than makes up for it in modality and resilience, as the channel effect is notoriously hard to interact with. Plus, it can be a big ol' turtle in the late game if need be.
- [Emeritus of Abundance](about:blank): Has quickly become a personal favorite of mine, the fact that it prepares on ETB lets you drop this down a turn early to avoid burning a spot in your hand, and then cast the regrowth whenever needed. Plus, the fact that it's a 3 MV 3/4 with vigilance means it's a surprisingly good early attacker and blocker. And remember all of the discussion in the ramp section about extra lands? You'll find this being able to re-prepare itself repeatedly each game (assuming it sticks around long enough). You haven't heard people truly groan until you [assassin's trophy](about:blank) an opponent's permanent, attack with the Emeritus, and then regrowth back the assassin's trophy to do it again. Overall, while this is significantly slower than [Eternal Witness](about:blank) I feel like the beefier body and ability to use whenever (and repeatedly during the mid/late game) gives this a solid advantage over Ewit.
- [Timeless Witness](about:blank): Same as Ewit, has the benefit/downside of being 4 MV so you can cast it off the top with Glarb. Plus being able to surveil it away and then pay the externalize cost to get it back is always great.

Specific Recursion:

- [Shifting Woodland](about:blank): a land that can turn into whatever permanent we want in our graveyard? Sign me up! This has saved me multiple times by becoming a copy of an essential win condition like [Polluted Cistern](about:blank), [Doom Whisperer](about:blank), or [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank), or becoming a great attacker by copying [Uro, Titan of Nature's Wrath](about:blank) or [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank). Some seriously cool tech you can do with this is have it become a copy of [Carpet of Flowers](about:blank) to have it become a pseudo-fast mana piece (assuming there's enough islands in play to have it be mana positive). Don't forget that it can tap itself for mana to pay for it's own cost! Opportunities are endless with this.
- [Reanimate](about:blank): While I did put the disclaimer at the beginning of the section, this deck still benefits from having a cheap reanimation spell, and what better option than Reanimate! It can be a cheap copy of a 3 drop, cheat out an early beater, or better yet snipe off someone else's [Valgavoth, Terror Eater](about:blank)! Possibilities are endless with this.
- [Dim Oubliette](about:blank): the less important side of this room but still useful, can fill our graveyard briefly and bring back a key creature. Worth noting that since the room is an overall MV 7 spell, you can cast either side off the top of your library with Glarb out in play for the respective room cost, but otherwise not much else to say.
- [Dread Return](about:blank): MVP reanimate option for Glarb, is a 4 MV spell so you can cast this off the top with Glarb out on the field and then flash it back when you have a couple of dorks or chumps that you can spare.
- [Living Death](about:blank): Great board wipe and reanimation piece, really shines when you've been able to fill your graveyard and also exile off your opponents. Plus, the fact that it sacrifices helps get around anything pesky with indestructible.
- [The Scarab God](about:blank): I've been back-and-forth on this guy, but I think he's gonna ultimately stay in. While he is a bit of an investment at 5 MV, the effect you get off of him is great. First, he's hard to remove and sticks around during the game, going back to your hand if he gets picked off (barring an exile effect). The real money is in the second effect, letting you dump your mana to snipe off anything in your graveyard or your opponent's graveyard at instant speed. You can combine this with [Wilderness Reclamation](about:blank) to really get an engine going, and it adds a great amount of redundancy and resilience to your gameplan. Plus, you can do some really dumb things like killing your own [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank) to bring it back the same turn. This card is usually in and out of my deck constantly, but in games where it has stuck around and I have enough mana it has more than paid for itself.

Removal and Interaction

![My Favorite GIF](https://media3.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExZGg2ZHFmZmkwa3BscmRwdXk3cDFjN2JyZWt0dGh6czdnMXUwZmZydyZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/yr7n0u3qzO9nG/giphy.gif)

Here's where we get into the fun part of this deck.

I loooooove having removal and interaction, nothing gets me going more than games where it's a brawl on the stack. As such I've tried to implement some great options for interaction that can help you snipe off key pieces from others and protect your own game plan. I've also thrown in some fun and personal favorites.

Stack Interaction and Counterspells:

- [Counterbalance](about:blank): yeah this card is nuts with Glarb. Being able to know what your top card is, and then surveil to sculpt for a chance to counter something is truly evil and has won me many games in tempo alone. I know this deck isn't designed to make full use out of this, but just with Glarb it's really strong, and then when you pair with with [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) it becomes impossible to break.
- [Counterspell](about:blank): the OG, not much to say here.
- [An Offer You Can’t Refuse](about:blank): cheap and efficient interaction, better used defensively to protect your pieces or stop countermagic than offensively since it gives your opponents treasures. Have preferred this over Mana Sculpt as it is significantly cheaper and I can still have some mana left over after developing my board.
- [Sink into Stupor](about:blank): MDFC that can enter untapped AND counter/return permanent to hand? Amazing include, hard to beat. Also gets around anything with a "can't be countered" clause.
- [Mindbreak Trap](about:blank): Can be cast off the top with Glarb, blows out storm decks, and can also be cast for free? Another amazing include. Some people may give you crap since it's a "signpost card" for high power, but the utility and synergy more than justifies this. Also gets around any "can't be countered" clause.
- [Ertai Resurrected](about:blank): I've been sleeping on this for a while, but this has quickly become a favorite in my deck. The flexibility to kill a creature or counter a spell OR ability is fantastic, and at flash speeds gives this an edge over something like [Ravenous Chupacabra](about:blank). The only downside is that it can give your opponent a card which isn't great, but the usefulness is just too good to pass up. Cool tech with this is that anything that reanimates/blinks at instant speed can make this into another counterspell if it's in your graveyard.

Single/Multi-target Removal and Interaction

- [Assassin's Trophy](about:blank): premier removal at the cost of giving them a land, not bad at all.
- [Baleful Mastery](about:blank): can be cast off the top with Glarb for the at the expense of giving someone else a draw, but note that it doesn't have to be the owner of the permanent you're removing! Having the ability to exile a creature rather than destroying it comes up more often that you'd think, there are a lot of indestructible heaters out there like [Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger](about:blank). Also feel free to swap this with [Deadly Rollick](about:blank), it's not in here just for price (if I could find the budget for it in this deck I would absolutely include it) but is a solid inclusion for the same reasons.
- [Archdruid's Charm](about:blank): Okay, this one is interesting. On paper it seems like an odd inclusion since its (in theory pretty hard to cast early game) and <4 mana (can't be cast off the top with Glarb), BUT the benefits far outweigh the difficulty in casting it.

- - Creature/Land tutor: Can grab your key mid game bomb, early set up piece, or late game finisher. Or, can snipe off a graveyard with a [Bojuka Bog](about:blank), or if you include it back in, can phase out a creature with [Talon Gates of Madara](about:blank).
- - +1/+1 counter and damage: with Glarb out in play, this becomes a poor man's [Legolas's Quick Reflexes](about:blank) and can kill any creature without indestructible. Plus, this buffs up your frog to be an even more menacing blocker, what’s not to love?
- - Artifact/Enchantment exile: not much to say here, solid hate piece to remove anything notoriously difficult to remove like [The One Ring](about:blank), [Blightsteel Colossus](about:blank), [Darksteel Forge](about:blank), etc.
- - Also personal note, I haven't had that much issue with the requirement as I typically cast this during the midgame after having my lands and mana developed. Fair warning though that mileage may vary!

- [Colossal Skyturtle](about:blank): instant speed channel to bounce someone's creature? sweet! IMPORTANT TO NOTE that you CANNOT channel off the top of your library, but is still an amazing include.
- [Fell the Profane](about:blank): 4 mana MDFC that you can cast off the top for either the land side (which can also enter untapped) or cast to kill a creature or planeswalker? Sweet! I see the bolt MDFCs as basically upgrades to basics, since they tap for only 1 color and can enter untapped if need be.
- [Force of Vigor](about:blank): Can be cast off the top for free (still have to exile a green spell), but is a great removal piece as it hits 2 for the price of one. Since this deck is more on the control-side of things, having cheap multi-target removal is great.
- [Snuff Out](about:blank): can cast for free off the top of your library at the cost of 4 life? That's basically just free removal!

True Spite Pieces (both have been tentatively cut):

- [Mind Twist](about:blank): interaction check! Do they have a redirect or counterspell? If not they functionally lose, or at the very least lose so much tempo that they're basically out of the game. With Glarb you can also cast this off the top if you choose X=3 to have the overall mana cost be 4 or greater. Prepare to lose friends over this, but also win games!
- [Volatile Stormdrake](about:blank): [Prosper, Tome-Bound](about:blank) players hate to see this coming. This creature is so insane I don't even know where to start. You can drop this on turn 2 to just completely fuck over someone and steal their commander (out of spite or value that's entirely your call), or can you can drop this in later in the game to either steal a key value piece or remove a difficult creature. Don't forget that you don't have to spend the energy to keep it and can just let it die/get sacrificed. Also insane when paired with [Chord of Calling](about:blank) as you can play this at instant speed for X=2, which no one expects. Plus, the fact that this has hexproof from activated and triggered abilities makes this notoriously hard to stop. Important to note though that you generally want to pair this with some type of edict or removal spell to also kill the stormdrake once they have it and send it back to YOUR graveyard so you can reanimate it/recur it to cast it again. I've had multiple people scoop it up after I [Volatile Stormdrake](about:blank) their permanent into a [Sheoldred](about:blank) ETB to get the stormdrake back in my graveyard. If you have the budget feel free to also add in a [Gilded Drake](about:blank).

Wincons and Combos

![My Favorite GIF](https://media4.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExMnNjcGxvNGxsb3o2ejJvNDBvNGl0dWk2cGRqMWQ5dmo2eDJ0ZGduaiZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/8vcYslf4dQU6qvFSFv/giphy.gif)

What I struggle with the most in making a deck: figuring out how to win! Although I think I've figured it out for this one.

This deck does look to piece together some mid game bombs to push the game to a win and help close it out. As for combos, my goal was to include something where the pieces were individually synergistic with the deck, and that when put together could churn out into a win (kind of like something incidental you'd see in an aristocrats list with something like [Pitiless Plunderer](about:blank) and [Warren Soultrader](about:blank)).

For the primary "combo" of the deck we have [Polluted Cistern](about:blank), [Doom Whisperer](about:blank), and [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank).

- [Doom Whisperer](about:blank): an absolute powerhouse of a card in this deck. Stat wise, it's a 5MV 6/6 with flying and trample, meaning it can put belt to ass by itself in a game to whittle down life totals. The real juice is in the activated ability, letting you pay life to dig through your library as many times as you'd like (life total and cards in library withstanding). This means that you can dig for a counterspell, removal, etc. whenever you want, and use what you send to the graveyard to turn and win the game. It’s like a budget [necropotence](about:blank), functionally giving the same rate of 1 life:1 card (albeit it doesn't go to your hand). I just love this guy overall, he’s usually my go-to tutor target with something like [chord of calling](about:blank) or [nature’s rhythm](about:blank) since getting him out sooner will allow you to start searching for the remaining pieces of your deck.
- [Polluted Cistern](about:blank): this is how you convert your surveils to damage, will ping each opponent for 1 based on what card type hits the graveyard. So ideally, you want to mill one-by-one or ensure that whatever you dump has multiple card types to maximize damage. Combined with [Doom Whisperer](about:blank), you can just dump your whole library to deal as much damage as you have cards in your library to your opponents, each time at the cost of your health. However, this becomes a lot easier when you combine it with...
- [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank): lets you convert opponent's life loss to your life gain. When you assemble all three pieces, each surveil trigger from Doom Whisperer should make you net positive on life, and allow you to dump everything at instant speed and on top of any other trigger/piece of interaction sent your way. While there is a scenario where you don't have enough cards in your library to burn people out, at that point you've already lost the game or have another way to convert into a win. Also same as Doom Whisperer, this acts as a big body that can pressure life totals on its own. Also individually serves to help stabilize your life total. Crazy when combined with [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank).

In games that have gone long or that I have multiple tutors/significant card draw, I typically look to assemble this line and win from here. Plus, getting out Doom Whisperer early lets you start investing life into your library and search for the remaining pieces a lot easier as well. Couple this with all the recursion and creature tutors and you have a fairly resilient and consistent way to try and win the game.

However, if this gets heavily interrupted or you're looking for another way to close out the games, you also have these options:

Alternative Win Conditions

- [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank): Fantastic top-end finisher. While it is a bit costly at 8 mana, it gives you an instant effect on ETB to help stabilize and thin out your opponents. And once the game has transitioned to a 1v2 or 1v1, this card becomes exceptionally brutal to play against, hitting their hand and board each turn while helping to develop your own. And since we have a handful of efficient reanimate spells and effects, this can also be cheated out early game. While this can get stuck in your hand for a while if drawn into early game, having an early Glarb out can prevent this and help pitch it to your graveyard.
- [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank): Can win games on its own when paired with a well timed board wipe or mass-reanimation effect. This knight puts in so much work, even when there isn't that much reanimation present in the deck. Being able to burn others on self-mill and use his mana sink to convert excess mana to a win is fantastic, and can also hate out any top-deck tutors that opponents play. Anyone with an [Oracle of Mul Daya](about:blank) hates to see this guy coming. Fun tech you can do is fill your entire graveyard with like 20 creatures and then [Bojuka Bog](about:blank) yourself to kill everyone else. Have done this like 5 times to win games.
- [Massacre Wurm](about:blank): Creature token decks hate to see this guy coming. This guy has also single-handedly won me games by blowing out token players and wiping off incidental dorks. Fantastic hate piece and card overall.
- [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank): Drop this guy down after a couple of your other creatures and the game is basically in the bag for you. Most of your win conditions are super black-pip heavy, so you'll get a lot of value out of this guy each time you drop him (speaking from experience). This gets really crazy when you also drop down a clone afterwards like [Phyrexian Metamorph](about:blank) or [Clever Impersonator](about:blank), or even more nuts when you pair it with [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank) as you just gain double the life.
- [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank): Banger of a card, can win you the game on the spot if not shift the game so far in your favor that it's basically over. Combined with Syr Konrad will almost guarantee a win. Treat this more of a value overwhelm card than a specific way to reanimate a bomb from your graveyard. Absolutely brutal vs opposing graveyard decks since it snipes off their best creature as well, just be careful that you don't set up their graveyard for them. Always good to have a piece of graveyard hate in your back pocket just in case after resolution of this spell.
- [Jetsam](about:blank): An absolute Sultai gem, this has also won me games all by itself. At first glance, you may think this UNCOMMON is fairly low rate, as you only get to mill each opponent for three and then cast a spell from the three milled. Then you reread the card, and realize that it doesn't specify that you can only cast from the three milled, and that you can actually just cast ANY SPELL from their graveyard, including the three milled. You can snipe off removal, creatures, draw, or whatever you want with this card, with the rule that it must be in their graveyard when you cast this. However, come turn 7 when everyone's developed their board and has been wiped once and you'll see the true power of this card shine.
- [The Scarab God](about:blank): Not much to say here regarding it being a wincon, it does offer some incidental burn but the main appeal of this is in the reanimate effect. It can help you get back your key combo pieces, cheat out your Archon and Masascre Wurm, and double up on your Gray Merchant.
- [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank): Not the most direct way to win, but beating down opponents with a steady stream of 2/2's isn't the worth thing in the world. Plus, it being a 5/5 flyer does lend itself to being a solid attacker/blocker. Don't forget you can surprise flip your manifested 2/2 into a 6/6 [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank) at instant speed whenever you have priority, done so as a SPECIAL ACTION (same timing/rules as tapping a LAND for MANA btw, opponents cannot respond to the flip of this).

Clones:

- [Phyrexian Metamorph](about:blank): Can be cast off the top with Glarb for and 2 life instead of the full cost, which is great. Note that this enters as a copy of the creature/artifact, so you DON'T have to specify what you're grabbing before it resolves, and it also goes around hexproof, shroud, etc.
- [Clever Impersonator](about:blank): Same notes as above, but it can enter as a copy of ANY permanent. Nice [Rhystic Study](about:blank), I think I'll have one as well!

Glarb survives this which is great, and you can use this to accelerate into a bigger mana spell like [Jetsam](about:blank) or [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) on that same turn. Graveyard Hate

Graveyard hate for thee, but not for me! It's always important to have enough ways to interact with opponent's graveyards, especially since we have a good chunk of ways to fill it up for them. The last thing we want to do is accelerate their gameplan faster than we can develop ours, and we certainly don't want to be succeptible to a silver bullet like [Valgavoth, Terror Eater](about:blank), so we need to have a decent amount of ways to hit graveyards. As a personal note, I would like to include some more graveyard hate and get this category up to around 6 pieces or so, but have had some trouble finding good options/slots to do so. Feel free to add more pieces as needed, don't take what I have as gospel.

Graveyard Hate:

- [Boggart Trawler](about:blank): [Bojuka Bog](about:blank) but as a creature, wow! Unironically better in my opinion since it can be tutored for and easily recurred. Is also really easy to pair with [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank) to turn your own graveyard into a nuclear bomb.

- [Dauthi Voidwalker](about:blank): The newest addition to the group. This provides great one-sided grave hate for you opponents and also stifles any death triggers. Is somewhat of a non-bo when you cast things like [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) or [Jetsam](about:blank), but you can sacrifice it on your own term to have graveyards “turn back on”.

Protection & Flash Enablers

![My Favorite GIF](https://media0.giphy.com/media/v1.Y2lkPTc5MGI3NjExbmVoMXhhdHFjZXA5aGcxY2VuYjY1ZjUxamxzZGVxdGhkcDVwYzJqZiZlcD12MV9pbnRlcm5hbF9naWZfYnlfaWQmY3Q9Zw/dTvIWbJcbY9LoNz0gr/giphy.gif)

Wanted to also include some pieces I have for protection that enables our key creatures and flash enablers that can change the dynamic of our deck.

Protection:

- [Plaza of Heroes](about:blank): This land is especially great here as it "turns on" for rainbow colors as soon as Turn 2/3 when you get Glarb out.
- [Delighted Halfling](about:blank): Nice to protect Glarb and some other important legendaries
- [Lightning Greaves](about:blank): Good to protect Glarb and enable his surveil ability the same turn he's dropped. Also works to enable "titan" creatures like [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank) and [Uro, Titan of Nature's Wrath](about:blank) the same turn that they drop. Have been 50-50 on if I need a redundant piece like [Swiftfoot Boots](about:blank).
- [Dauthi Voidwalker](about:blank): From gameplay, I’ve realized that this deck really wants more proactive pieces that both develop your board and provide some degree of protection/impact. Previous iterations have focused more on having available mana to interact with others and protect wins, but in reality this devolved into trying to police the table and hold up mana to interact with others rather than develop my own board, setting me behind in tempo and resulting in the constant feeling of not having any direction during the mid game. I believe that pieces like Dauthi Voidwalker help close this gap as it not only hates out opponents but also stops them from playing consistent value spells in fear of fueling my options. This also acts as insurance for any of your interaction/stack wars as you can sac it to cast any counterspell or redirect that an opponent casts. Note that you still have to abide by timing restrictions, but flash enablers help mitigate this and give you more flexibility on what to cast. Also note that the cards are exiled with a void counter, so if you reanimate Dauthi (or if someone else plays him as well) you can still access those exiled cards.

Flash Enablers:

- [Alchemist's Refuge](about:blank): a bit of a mana sink each time, but a repeatable [Emergence Zone](about:blank) is nothing to scoff at. Can allow for very interesting plays on opponent's turns, or for extra gas on your own turn over top anything on the stack.
- [High Fae Trickster](about:blank): Same as [Alchemist's Refuge](about:blank), but just requires more of an upfront sink. This can be flashed in off the top as well, and can also be tutored for with [Waterlogged Teachings](about:blank). Super useful creature to have out on the field, and works exceptionally well when paired with...
- [Wilderness Reclamation](about:blank): Poor man's [Seedborn Muse](about:blank), I didn't get the hype until I played it myself. Being able to do whatever you want on your turn and still have mana up to interact for the rest of the turn cycle is just crazy, and when paired with a flash enabler like [High Fae Trickers](about:blank) just doubles your mana and puts you so far ahead of everyone else tempo-wise. If you want to include gamechangers in your Bracket 3 deck (like a normal person), [Seedborn Muse](about:blank) will go crazy here.
- [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](about:blank): Newest addition to the group. 5 mana and 3 blue is a significant investment, but listen when I tell you that it REALLY pays off. Sultai doesn't get many protective pieces like White, without access to options like [Grand Abolisher](about:blank) and [Silence](about:blank). However, 5feri just puts a mental beatdown on your opponents, protecting your turn, stopping them from doing combat tricks, and also protecting any of your interaction. That's right! If you cast anything at flash speed, they can't respond back at flash speed! And the fact that this also gives your 26+ creatures flash, while itself also having flash (so you can drop this on someone's end step) is just fantastic. I was initially considering [Tidal Barracuda](about:blank) but then I realized that this is quite a terrorist card and can REALLY bite you in the ass when you play vs someone like [Fire Lord Azula](about:blank). Flash responsibly.

Tutors

I used to not be the biggest fan of tutors in commander, but that was because I kept tutoring for the same thing over and over again. With this deck, now that I have a variety of options and silver bullets to choose from, these tutors only serve to accelerate and speed up my game plan (not that it lets me get my piece out a turn sooner, but rather it saves me the time and cards that I would normally need to dig for the piece by itself).

Tutors:

- [Lim Dul's Vault](about:blank): grab a drink and some chips, we're gonna be here for a while. This 2 mana tutor is fantastic with Glarb out on the field, as it lets you sculpt your perfect 5 as you dig through your library. The only thing to consider is that out of respect for your time and everyone else, please think of what you want to grab ahead of time. Since you can look through your entire library, it's very easy to get paralysis by analysis.
- [Nature's Rhythm](about:blank): Important to note, you can cast this off the top with Glarb if X=2 or more (total MV of the spell >= 4). Can grab whatever you need, and then can be harmonized from your graveyard. Just a great tutor overall
- [Chord of Calling](about:blank): same as [Nature's Rhythm](about:blank) but at instant speed, another banger of a tutor.
- [Archdruid's Charm](about:blank): already discussed in the **Removal and Interaction** section, but can just grab whatever you need like the other discussed tutors.
- [Waterlogged Teachings](about:blank): surprisingly a fantastic tutor, while the MDFC land side is nothing impressive the fact that it can grab creatures with Flash is a great addition. It's expensive to just grab any random piece of interaction, sure, but it can easily fetch you some draw with [The Unagi of Kyoshi Island](about:blank) or a flash enabler/way to win the game with [High Fae Trickster](about:blank). Plus it can also fetch your other tutors, which is a nice plus.

## Mulligans and Overall Gameplan

Mulligans

Since [Glarb, Calamity's Augur](about:blank) is so great at fixing draws, lands, and filling your graveyard, you generally want to look for some combination of cards that gives you 3 mana by Turn 3. This can be 2 lands and a mana dork/1-mana ramp piece or 3 lands. I also don't mind keeping a 4 or 5 land hand, since I can just use Glarb to dig for some gas afterwards, and use the lands in hand to setup for future turns. As for what else to look for in your hand, generally any piece of draw or setup is great since you want to start searching and preparing for your 5+ MV bombs. Any hand with [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) and 3 lands is an automatic keep, same with something like [Sylvan Library](about:blank) or [Ripples of Undeath](about:blank) since they help you just dig through so much of your library. Also 3 lands plus [Prismatic Undercurrents](about:blank) is usually an auto-keep.

The biggest takeaway from this is that Glarb can functionally bail you out from a bad hand. You can absolutely keep a “riskier” hand with something like 2 lands and an [Exploration](about:blank), but understand that Glarb gives you access to your library and to whatever else you need.

IMPORTANT NOTE: You can be susceptible to early aggro, be sure to mulligan properly for early interaction if need to be to get the heat off of you. Glarb does act as a great deterrent, but people will absolutely call your bluff and still full swing at you to chip out at your life. If people are developing early swingers like [Ainok Strike Leader](about:blank) don't be afraid to chump Glarb to save yourself a lot of life in the long run. Better to spend 5 mana next turn to recast Glarb than be down 10 life on Turn 4. Be smart about what to drop and when, you can always bounce back in the mid-late game.

Overall Gameplan and Closing Comments

As outlined in the Mulligan section, you really just want Glarb out, hopefully on Turn 2 but no later than Turn 3.

Assuming Glarb sticks on Turn 3, from Turns 4-6 you’re just looking to setup and stabilize. This is usually done by having some combination of ramp, a draw/value engine, and a removal piece or two in hand. With your density of draw, digging, and tutors, you can start to assemble your key engine pieces like [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) and [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank) to setup for the late game.

Key early/mid game pieces to look for:

- [Talion, the Kindly Lord](about:blank): super solid drop on like Turn 4 to draw and burn out others
- [Uro, Titan of Nature’s Wrath](about:blank): helps you accelerate out a land and gives you a piece to build up towards and play around (escaping him out and using him to beat out others)
- [Enduring Vitality](about:blank): early beater/blocker, helps setup for big mana turns later in the game

If you did everything right, by Turn 6 you should have a healthy amount of lands on the battlefield, a good amount of cards in your graveyard, and a couple of the aforementioned "bombs/wincons" in your hand or in play. From there, you can generally play the rest of the game by feel, there isn't any sort of formula or specific gameplan you're looking to assemble other than the incidental combo outlined above.

This deck does play kind of “toolbox-y”, so you will normally try to fetch or find the specific creature to best fuck over your opponents and put you in an advantageous spot. Vs a sacrifice or token heavy deck? Can dig for [Massacre Wurm](about:blank) to get around their token slop to hit their key piece. At the very end when you’ve been able to develop your board, you can drop down [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](about:blank) to chunk people out on life (works even better when you chain a couple of clones with this). Note that this deck does use quite a bit of life as a resource to feed your [Doom Whisperer](about:blank), and people will start to attack you to put pressure on your board. You can always dig for [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank), [Archon of Cruelty](about:blank), or [Uro, Titan of Nature’s Wrath](about:blank) to try and stabilize and swing life totals in your favor again.

One of the strongest lines of play you can do is cheat out [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank) via a tutor like [Nature's Rhythm](about:blank) or [Chord of Calling](about:blank), and then pair that up with something like [Syr Konrad, the Grim](about:blank) to drain people out consistently or something like [Enduring Vitality](about:blank) to accelerate your mana by 3 dorks a turn cycle (on top of whatever else you have on board). [Abhorrent Occulus](about:blank) is just a crazy tempo card, and people will sometimes let your tutor slip through since they don't expect you to grab out this tempo monster for only X=3.

From my own experience, this deck can very easily set up and start piecing together some advantage from Turns 1-4 just by getting out a mana dork, Glarb, and a value engine going. I'm still working through some kinks on the mid-game side of things, but I promise that once you're able to get out [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) or an early [Abhorrent Oculus](about:blank) the game can very easily fall into your lap. Your density of creature tutors should help you find the pieces that you need and setup for a combo win or value overwhelm once you have an advantage.

Some games will end up just feeling like:

- Turns 1-4: Ramp and setup, good value pieces on board and a promising future.
- Turns 5-8: ???
- Turns 9+: Pull a win out of my ass

Some weaknesses of this deck:

- Notoriety. While Glarb himself doesn't attract a lot of attention, a good chunk of the permanents and spells that you run will. So be prepared to draw the table's ire and have to fight off multiple opponents. Channel your inner politician, show your hand, and do what you need to do to survive.
- Early Aggression. This deck can really feel the squeeze when taking some hits early on. You'll be pumping life into your [Doom Whisperer](about:blank) and something like [Lim-Dul's Vault](about:blank) to find the pieces you need, so every bit helps. It is shocking how much you can stabilize with something like [Bloodthirsty Conqueror](about:blank), but this can also have the inverse effect of everyone decided to focus you down. Be smart about what you play and when, and try to have some sort of interaction or protection in hand.
- Speed. From my experience and games, I can normally close out around Turn 8-10, but this is entirely dependent on how much you're interacted with. Last few bouts of games have gone to like Turn 10-11, so mileage may vary. You can very easily goldfish and piece together your "combo" win by turn 6 or 7, but in reality people will absolutely start to target you and remove off your key pieces. I am actively working on speeding this deck up and raising the floor, which from my change-logs has seen me cut a lot more of my top end in favor of more interaction and sweepers.
- Mana. Surprisingly enough, I have felt starved for mana many times while playing this deck, just being shy a mana or two to try and push for a lead or interact back on other people's turns. Getting out an early extra-land piece and also ensuring you hit your lands drops each turn is ESSENTIAL to having a good game with Glarb, but fortunately the commander inherently helps fix your draws.

In the future, I may slowly start to pivot this deck from less of a "value grind slop pile" with an incidental combo to something more focused and combo-driven/oriented, but for now I'm having some fun with the overall playstyle. This deck does feel incredibly resilient and there's no better feeling than dropping down a big [Breach the Multiverse](about:blank) or [Jetsam](about:blank) to win you the game. Just the other day I was able to [Jetsam](about:blank) someone's [Emrakul, the World Anew](about:blank) and push for a win off that. Speed and the expected turn to win does leave something to be desired though.

The bones of this deck are definitely there and I'm sure there's a version of this list down the line that has "solved" a lot of the aforementioned issues.

Feel free to check out the Considering pile below for any other fun cards that I've "considered" putting in this deck or have tried out any removed, and drop down any comments below if you have any questions.
